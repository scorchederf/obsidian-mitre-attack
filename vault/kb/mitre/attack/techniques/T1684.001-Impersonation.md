## Description

Adversaries may impersonate a trusted person or organization in order to persuade and trick a target into performing some action on their behalf. For example, adversaries may communicate with victims (via [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1598-Phishing_for_Information\|Phishing for Information]], [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1566-Phishing\|Phishing]], or [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1534-Internal_Spearphishing\|Internal Spearphishing]]) while impersonating a known sender such as an executive, colleague, or third-party vendor. Established trust can then be leveraged to accomplish an adversaryâ€™s ultimate goals, possibly against multiple victims.<br><br>In many cases of business email compromise or email fraud campaigns, adversaries use impersonation to defraud victims -- deceiving them into sending money or divulging information that ultimately enables [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1657-Financial_Theft\|Financial Theft]].<br><br>Adversaries will often also use social engineering techniques such as manipulative and persuasive language in email subject lines and body text such as `payment`, `request`, or `urgent` to push the victim to act quickly before malicious activity is detected. These campaigns are often specifically targeted against people who, due to job roles and/or accesses, can carry out the adversaryâ€™s goal.â€Żâ€Ż<br><br>Impersonation is typically preceded by reconnaissance techniques such as [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1589-Gather_Victim_Identity_Information\|Gather Victim Identity Information]] and [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1591-Gather_Victim_Org_Information\|Gather Victim Org Information]] as well as acquiring infrastructure such as email domains (i.e. [[kb/mitre/attack/techniques/T1583.001-Domains\|Domains]]) to substantiate their false identity.[^1] <br><br>There is the potential for multiple victims in campaigns involving impersonation.
